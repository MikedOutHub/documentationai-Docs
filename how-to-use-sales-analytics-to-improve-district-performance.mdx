## Overview

Sales reports are not just paperwork — they are the playbook for making smarter decisions and finding hidden growth. When you look at a sales report, you are seeing customer behavior in action. Which items sell fastest, what times of day bring in the most business, and where profits are slipping all live inside those reports.

Stores that analyze sales reports regularly see growth 10 to 15 percent faster than those that only check occasionally.

## Focus on the Right Metrics

Not every number matters equally. Track these four metrics first:

- **Top-selling items** — what drives most of your sales

- **Basket size** — average value per transaction

- **Category performance** — snacks, beverages, tobacco, prepared foods

- **Margins** — which items bring the most profit

These metrics help you understand both what sells and what actually pays the bills.

<Callout kind="tip" collapsed="false">
  Your sales reports are not just numbers — they are customer behavior written down. A slow snack category is not just a data point. It is a signal that placement, assortment, or promotion needs to change.
</Callout>

## Spot Opportunities and Weaknesses

Look for patterns in your reports:

- Are drinks spiking during certain hours?

- Is a category slowing down compared to last month?

- Are margins declining in a specific department?

- Is basket size dropping on certain shifts or days?

This analysis tells you where to double down and where to adjust. A slow snack category might mean you need better placement or a bundled promotion. A margin drop in beverages might signal a supplier price change you missed.

## Turn Reports Into Action

### Create a Weekly Review Habit

Set aside time every week to review reports. Even 20 minutes is enough to catch trends before they become problems. Do not wait for monthly closings to discover issues you could have fixed three weeks earlier.

### Use Reports as a Feedback Loop

Think of your reports as a test-and-learn system:

1. Try a new display, promotion, or product placement

2. Check the numbers before and after

3. If it works, repeat it and scale it

4. If it fails, move on quickly

<Callout kind="danger" collapsed="false">
  Managers who skip weekly report reviews are always reacting — cutting promotions that are actually working, keeping displays that are draining margin, and missing the 10% growth sitting right inside their own data. Weekly reviews are not optional for profitable stores.
</Callout>

## What to Look for by Category

- **Fuel** — monitor volume and margin separately; fuel drives traffic but inside sales drive profit

- **Beverages** — watch for hourly spikes that signal promotion or placement opportunities

- **Snacks and candy** — high margin category often underinvested in reporting attention

- **Tobacco** — consistent demand but tight margins; any decline needs immediate investigation

- **Prepared food** — track spoilage and waste alongside sales to get true margin picture

## Key Principle

Sales reports are a roadmap. By focusing on the right metrics, spotting opportunities, and acting quickly, you transform raw numbers into strategies that drive growth. Managers who use the weekly review cycle consistently turn reports into measurable sales lifts.
